Hallstatt Culture (Ha A - D)

Summary

Hallstatt culture, named after its type site.

Categorized as:

  • Widely farming communities
  • Highly-advanced metal-working
  • Long-range trade within the area and with Mediterranean cultures towards end of Hallstatt
  • Tribal based (little is known)
  • Villages
    • Only a few, such as Heuneburg in the south of Germany, were considered towns rather than villages

A period emphasizing social distinction, in an age of clashing elite classes of chieftains and warriors.

Languages

Most if not all languages spoken were Celtic-type.

Civilizations

Categorized as:

  • Forts situated on hill tops
  • Approximately the size of a small modern village
  • Workshops of bronze-, silver-, and goldsmiths

Burials

Important persons had large graves, sometimes a chamber with a hill was build upon them called a tumuli. Inside of these there were weapons, pottery, jewellery, and other items of importance. Occasionally, there were chariot burials, where a chariot - with the horses - was buried with them.

Trade

Trade with Greece was common, proven through finding black ink art at burial sites. Trade items included amber, ivory, and wine.

Hallstatt Zones

Western Hallstatt Zone

Primary zones:

  • Northeastern France: Champagne-Ardenne, Lorraine, Alsace
  • Northern Switzerland: Swiss plateau
  • Southern Germany: Much of Swabia and Bavaria
  • Western Czech Republic: Bohemia
  • Western Austria: Vorarlberg, Tyrol, Salzkammergut

Side areas:

  • Central and North Italy: Po valley, Liguria, Venetia, Marche, Abruzzo, Friuli
  • Northern, Western, and Central Spain: Galicia, Asturias, Extremadura, Castile, Cantabria
  • Northern and North-Central Portugal: Minho, Douro, Tras-os-Montes, Beira Alta

Eastern Hallstatt Zone

Primary zones:

  • Eastern Austria: Lower Austria, Upper Styria
  • Eastern Czech Republic: Moravia
  • Southwestern Slovakia: Danubian Lowland
  • Western Hungary: Little Hungarian Plain
  • Eastern Slovenia: Hallstatt Archaeological Site in Vace (at the border between Lower Styria and Lower Carniola regions), Novo Mesto
  • Northern Croatia: Hrvatsko Zagorje, Istria
  • Northern and Central Serbia
  • Parts of Southwestern Poland

Pre-Roman Gaul (Hallstatt C-D)

There is little information on Pre-Roman Gauls aside from what has been gathered through archaeology, mainly from coins. But, as stated in the summary, they lived in small tribal farming villages, were highly-advanced (metal workers) and proficient traders with many (especially after Roman contact).
