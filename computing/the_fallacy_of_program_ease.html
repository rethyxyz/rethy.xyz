The Fallacy of Program Ease

Created: 2021-04-17 | Modified: 2021-07-16

Computer people know the notion that some of us want something that "just werkz". Usually this refers to either an operating system like Windows or Mac OS, or a program like VSCode or an App Store. This is a false notion.

Take Windows as an example: Windows is easy to jump into. You don't need preparation, or knowledge about its internals. It's easy, understandable, simplistic, flat in aesthetic, and thematically similar to real life objects, allowing you to understand things at face value. It isn't weird text like those Unix systems... You can immediately understand what is happening on the screen if you spend time reading whatever is presented to you.

This is great at the start. You can easily start working right away. No time spent on optimization. It's plug and play, so to speak. Download your IDE of choice and get at 'er.

This "just werkz" mentality is coated in diminishing returns. It's a trade off. You're trading initial setup time for future production speed/the time spent working on something. You are at the will of the software manufacturers to make something that makes the most sense to you, though it never will, as they need to appeal to the lowest common denominator. Your Windows system is made for illiterate babies, and technologically illiterate Grandmas, as they are the lowest common denominator...

If you install something from scratch, something that's truly simple in operation (such as *nix + a window manager (WM)), and understand how to use it, something that's meant to be fast, not dumbed down for the masses: You will work faster, and effectively, in the long run. You will not lose time, as you're trading your time to learn it now, for less time wasted later.

Look at programmers nowadays: Mac OS is commonly used. This seems to be the dominating OS among FANG types. Very many of them spend time writing code in bloated IDEs that have insane, ungodly keybindings completely without mnemonic, making it hard to build up muscle memory or guess what it may be without documentation. They use these programs as it's easy to jump into. You have a side bar with big labeled boxes like Project, Addons, Word Search, etc. There's a thick menu bar on top with everything you would (n)ever need that you can click on or attempt to invoke using Ctrl+k + v or some other weird key combo shit like that. An issue, is clicking stuff will never equal a good workflow. You're never committing it in your memory as you would with physical keyboard based bindings. If you spend real time learning an effective tool (like vim), you will save an astonishing amount of time in the future.

Look at this airplane dashboard. Do you see a screen with a mouse that has tiny, clickable boxes? No. That's because it would be inefficient. Buttons are permanent, fixed, and produces tactile feedback. You hit them, and work up muscle memory according to their location. Why should your computing experience be any different? How is it effective, if you're a programmer, to slowly be clicking things, and not developing upon a consistent workflow? If you buy a monitor of a different resolution, your mouse based workflow changes drastically. The locations of things are different, and you're going to be slow again.

Here's a real world example of how hard it is to learn computers when you put in the effort: My brother. Starting last month, he decided to start learning Linux with no prior experience. He decided on buying a used ThinkPad X220 (at around $180 CAD), and jump in right away. He said that he wanted to understand effective computing, as it obviously would be something important going into the future, and a good skill to have in general (all learning is good learning!). Him, being a recent college graduate from a Forestry-related program, had not long ago been introduced to the wonders of effective technological appliance through Excel formulas. He was able to process vast amounts of data from specific mathematical formulas, and in short time. He could see that doing this by hand instead would be ridiculous and redundant. As he knew from our conversations, that I was a proponent of the wonders of plain text data processing, not using proprietary file formats for backwards compatibility, and pro typesetting compilers (LaTeX and groff/troff). He was able to see the potential too.

Getting back to the point... He got his computer after a few weeks of transit, and I aided him in installing Arch Linux. You may think this is a dumb idea, as Arch is notorious for being a "hard" distro, mainly because of its lack of installer, but I don't think that's right. You should learn the hard way immediately than have to undo even more computer usage sins, such as hackjobs, and software trickery conducted by WYSIWYG software. After the install, he was set. I installed my build of dwm, and taught him the basics of window movement, spawning, workspaces, pacman, etc. I demonstrated dmenu usage, terminal spawning, and command execution. Finally, I told him to learn vim tutor, and to watch Luke Smith's Bash tutorials (and anything else by Smith that he found interesting (no information is bad information when you're learning computing!)). Of course, he ran into a few snags that he said he spent hours on, but because he was able to come to me when really stuck in a hole, I was able to show him the fix. Things for him started to click after a few days of use, and he really was able to understand it all after a week. One week. That's it. The most important part of Linux is using your browser. If you have an error, search it up. Someone is bound to have ran into the same error as you at some point in time. It isn't the operating system that's difficult, it's unlearning the years of fake computing that's difficult.

Stop making shortcuts in your (computer) life, and go right to the source! Understand what you're doing. Don't be at the will of these babymode software manufacturers. Develop your workflow, and benefit from the speed and increased productivity in the future.
